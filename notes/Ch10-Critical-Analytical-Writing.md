# Ch10 Critical, Analytical Writing: Critical Thinking When Writing

## 1. Summary of the Chapter

This chapter translates critical thinking skills into the writing process. It covers structuring an argument (introduction, body, conclusion), using signposts and transitions, integrating sources effectively, and maintaining a critical tone throughout. A checklist for self‑editing is provided.

## 2. Core Concepts & Definitions

|Term|Definition (from the book)|My Paraphrase|
|---|---|---|
|**Thesis Statement**​|The central claim that the entire essay defends.|The one sentence that sums up my whole argument.|
|**Signposting**​|Using words/phrases to guide the reader through the argument.|“First…”, “However…”, “Therefore…” to show logical flow.|
|**Hedging**​|Using cautious language to avoid overclaiming.|“It appears that…” instead of “It is proven that…”.|

## 3. Main Arguments / Models

- Critical writing is not just describing what others said; it is positioning yourself in relation to them.
    
- Each paragraph should advance the argument, not just repeat information.
    
- Transitions between paragraphs must show logical progression, not just topical shift.

## 6. Critical Reflection

- **Strengths:**​ The checklist at the end is a practical tool for revision.
    
- **Weaknesses:**​ The chapter assumes a standard essay format; creative or non‑linear writing forms are not addressed.
