# Ch07 Does it Add Up? Identifying Flaws in the Argument

## 1. Summary of the Chapter

This chapter catalogues common logical fallacies—errors in reasoning that make an argument invalid or weak. It covers fallacies of relevance (e.g., _ad hominem_, _straw man_), fallacies of ambiguity (e.g., equivocation), and formal fallacies (e.g., affirming the consequent). The goal is to equip readers to detect flaws in others’ arguments and avoid them in their own writing.

## 2. Core Concepts & Definitions

|Term|Definition (from the book)|My Paraphrase|
|---|---|---|
|**Fallacy**​|An error in reasoning that renders an argument unsound.|A trick or mistake that makes the logic break down.|
|**Ad Hominem**​|Attacking the person instead of the argument.|“You’re wrong because you’re biased,” without addressing the evidence.|
|**Straw Man**​|Misrepresenting an opponent’s position to make it easier to attack.|Exaggerating or twisting someone’s view so you can knock it down.|
|**False Dilemma**​|Presenting only two options when more exist.|“Either you’re with us or against us.”|
|**Circular Reasoning**​|The conclusion is used as a premise.|“The Bible is true because it is the word of God” (which assumes God exists).|

## 3. Main Arguments / Models

- Fallacies are common in everyday discourse, media, and even academic writing.
    
- Spotting a fallacy does not automatically disprove the conclusion; it only shows the reasoning is flawed.
    
- The best defence against fallacies is to slow down and examine the logical structure step by step.
